PUT AN END TO HATE.

ANYTHING WRITTEN ONLINE can become viral and enduring. Cruel language can lead to bullying and violence. Positive words can lead to learning and growth.

We believe that the way we behave online should be no different from the way that we behave in person. We believe that whenever possible, we should say or write online only that we'd be willing to ay or write in person. We pledge to intervene when we observe others being attacked or discriminated against, and to work to foster responsibile and accountable online disclourse.

By the late 00's, online gossip sites JuicyCampus and College ACB had agined enormous popularity. These sites encourage visitors to post anonymous and oftentimes malicious information about their peers, causeing widespread cyberbullying and online character attacks such as asking students to rank "fattest sophmore girls" or list "closeted gays." This trend of posting anonymous and hurtful information online reached high schools and colleges across the country.

Rebecca continued to be bullied on anonymous forums such as ask.fm, Kik and Voxer. She was bombared with messages including, "Go kill yourself,"" and "Why are you still alive?" Rebecca took her life at age 12.

Today, hate speech continues to profilerate throughout the Internet, normalized in the form of YouTube comments, animated GIFs, and tweets. Online anonymity affords users a sense of security that fosters a culture of cruelty and bigotry. Our goal is to create a conversation about the consequences of hateful speech that rethinks how we communicate online. Social media is full of positive potential; we can tap into it by holding each other accountable.
