JOIN THE CAMPAIGN

While bullying is nothing new, technology amplifies the most destructive effects
of this behavior. Anonymity can create a false sense of security, and social
media allows hateful language to travel instantaneously over global networks.
While there are instances where anonymity can help protect people from
reprisal or protect their identity, it is often used to cloak hateful speech.


By signing the pledge below, we agree to advocate for more constructive and thoughtful communication online by:


  • Holding ourselves to higher standards when communicating online.
  • Whenever possible, only writing what we would sign our names to.
  • Intervening when we observe others bullied or attacked.

PUT AN END TO HATE.

ANYTHING WRITTEN ONLINE can become viral and enduring. Cruel language can lead to
bullying and violence. Positive words can lead to learning and growth.

We believe that the way we behave online should be no different from the way that we behave in person. We believe that whenever possible, we should say or write online only that we’d be willing to say or write in person. We pledge to intervene when we observe others being attacked or discriminated against, and to work to foster responsibile and accountable online disclourse.

By the late 00’s, online gossip sites JuicyCampus
and College ACB had agined enormous popularity.
These sites encourage visitors to post anonymous
and oftentimes malicious information about
their peers, causing widespread cyberbullying and
online character attacks such as asking
students to rank “fattest sophmore girls” or
list “closeted gays.” This trend of posting
anonymous and hurtful information online reached
high schools and colleges across the country.

SLUT

REBECCA ANN SEDWICK changed schools when her
mother discovered that she was being bullied online
by more than a dozen girls. Rebecca’s mom even
shut down her Facebook profile.

10.19.2000—09.09.2013

Rebecca continued to be bullied on anonymous forums
such as ask.fm, Kik and Voxer. She was bombared with
messages including, “Go kill yourself,” and
“Why are you still alive?”

Rebecca took her life at age 12.

Today, hate speech continues to profilerate
throughout the Internet, normalized in the form
of YouTube comments, animated GIFs, and tweets.
Online anonymity affords users a sense of
security that fosters a culture of cruelty and
bigotry. Our goal is to create a conversation
about the consequences of hateful speech that
rethinks how we communicate online. Social media
is full of positive potential; we can tap into
it by holding each other accountable.
